Out direction same as in

Matrix multiply vector acting like MLP, where all current dimensions are combined in different weights to produce each dimension in another space.

$$ ùêÄ ùêó \\ \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ y_3 \end{bmatrix} = ùêÄ \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} $$

  1. Matrix ùêÄ is like a function that takes as input ùêó and output ùêÄùêó.
  2. Typically, ùêÄùêó points in different direction from the input ùêó.
  3. Eigenvectors are those ùêó make ùêÄùêó parallels to ùêó.
  4. Parallel means scaling: ùêÄùêó = Œªùêó, where the multiplier Œª is eigenvalue.

Given a matrix ùêÄ, how to solve its eigenvalues Œª and eigenvectors ùêó?

  • If Œª = 0, ùêó solved from ùêÄùêó = 0 by elimination is ùüé, which is useless.

Some hints can be found through the following examples

Projection matrix

Given a projection matrix ùêè, a 3D vector is projected on to a plane.

  1. Eigenvectors ùêó should parallel to the projected vectors ùêèùêó. So the vectors located in the plane all are eigenvectors, as $ùêèùêó=ùêó$, with Œª=1.

  2. Vectors perpendicular to the plane statisfy: $ùêèùêó=0$, i.e., Œª=0.

  3. Those two sets of eigenvectors are perpendicular.

Permutation matrix

Given the 2D permutation matrix $ùêÄ=[^{0 \ 1}_{1 \ 0}]$, which vector can make ùêÄùêó=ùêó, i.e., identical after permutation.

  1. ùêó = $[^1_1]$, with Œª=1.

  2. As ùêÄ is a 2D matrix, there should be 2 eigenvalues. Which vector can statisfy Œª=-1 ?

    ùêó = $[^{-1}_1]$

  3. The 2 sets of eigenvectors ùêó are perpendicular as well.

  4. Fact: The sum of eigenvalues equals to the sum of elements on diagonal of ùêÄ.

    Here, there is 1 + (-1) = 0 + 0.

How to solve Œª, ùêó

Trick: Rearrange to ùêÄùêó - Œªùêó = 0.

If there is $(ùêÄ - Œªùêà)ùêó = 0$ and ùêó, a non-zero vector, becomes 0 after multiplied by (ùêÄ - Œªùêà), the (ùêÄ - Œªùêà) must be sigular: determinant is 0.

The formula $ùêÄ - Œªùêà = 0$ doesn’t include ùêó, so eigenvalues Œª can be solved first, as in the example below.

Plus multiple identity

Given a matrix ùêÄ = $[^{3 \ 1}_{1 \ 3}]$, to calculate Œª, solve:

$$ \begin{aligned} | ùêÄ - Œªùêà | = 0 \\ \begin{bmatrix} 3-Œª & 1 \\ 1 & 3-Œª \end{bmatrix} = 0 \\ (3-Œª)^2 -1 = 0 \\ Œª^2 - 6Œª + 8 = 0 \\ (Œª-4)(Œª-2) = 0 \end{aligned} $$

Two roots: Œª‚ÇÅ=4, Œª‚ÇÇ=2. Then solve the 2 sets of eigenvectors.

  1. For Œª‚ÇÅ=4,

    $$ \begin{aligned} (ùêÄ - Œªùêà)ùêó = 0 \\ \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix} ùêó = 0 \\ \end{aligned} $$

    By letting the free variable to 1, ùêó can be solved as $[^1_1]$.

    Hence, one of eigenvectors is $[^1_1]$

  2. For Œª‚ÇÇ=2,

    $$ \begin{aligned} (ùêÄ - Œªùêà)ùêó = 0 \\ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} ùêó = 0 \\ \end{aligned} $$

    Hence, one of eigenvectors is $[^{-1}_1]$

The sum of the two eigenvalues is the trace of ùêÄ, and their product is the determinant of ùêÄ.

Comparing the matrix $[^{3 \ 1}_{1 \ 3}]$ and the permutation matrix $[^{0 \ 1}_{1 \ 0}]$, there is:

A $[^{3 \ 1}_{1 \ 3}]$ $[^{0 \ 1}_{1 \ 0}]$
Œª 4 and 2 1 and -1
ùêó $[^1_1]$ and $[^{-1}_1]$ $[^1_1]$ and $[^{-1}_1]$

If $ùêÄùêó =Œªùêó$, then $(ùêÄ+3ùêà)ùêó= ùêÄùêó+3ùêàùêó = (Œª+3ùêà)ùêó$

That means, if ùêÄ plus 3ùêà, then eigenvalues Œª will plus 3ùêà, while eigenvectors doesn’t change.

Can’t generalize

However, th above property (for multiple identity) can’t be generalized to plusing an arbitrary matrix ùêÅ.

Matrix addition doesn’t imply eigenvalues addition, because the eigenvector of ùêÅ is typically not ùêó. Therefore, the following addition can’t be performed:

$$ ùêÄùêó =Œªùêó \\ ùêÅùêó =Œ±ùêó \\ (ùêÄ+ùêÅ)ùêó = (Œª+Œ±)ùêó $$

Complex eigenvalues

Considering rotation matrix:

$$ \begin{aligned} \begin{bmatrix} cosŒ∏ \\ sinŒ∏ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} cosŒ∏ & -sinŒ∏ \\ sinŒ∏ & cosŒ∏ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{aligned} $$

Let Œ∏ be 90¬∞, the rotation matrix is $[^{0 \ -1}_{1 \ 0}]$.

However, according to the theory “eigenvector outcomes in the direction that it went in”, there seems to be no eigenvector intuitively, as any vector gets in will rotate 90 degree by this matrix.

  1. Solve its eigenvalues:

    $$ \begin{aligned} | ùêÄ - Œªùêà | = 0 \\ |\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} Œª & 0 \\ 0 & Œª \end{bmatrix}| = \begin{vmatrix} -Œª & -1 \\ 1 & -Œª \end{vmatrix} = 0 \\ Œª^2 + 1 = 0 \end{aligned} $$

    Two roots: Œª‚ÇÅ=i, Œª‚ÇÇ=-i

    Anti-symmetric matrix has imaginary eigenvalues which are always in pairs, as they’re complex conjugate. While a symmetrix matrix’s eigenvalues are all real numbers.

  2. Solve eigenvectors ùêó:

    • For Œª‚ÇÅ=i,

      $$ \begin{aligned} (ùêÄ - Œªùêà) ùêó = 0 \\ \begin{bmatrix} 0-Œª‚ÇÅ & -1 \\ 1 & 0-Œª‚ÇÅ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = 0 \\ \begin{bmatrix} -i & -1 \\ 1 & -i \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = 0 \\ \end{aligned} $$

      Let the free variable b=1, then $[^a_b] = [^i_1]$

    • For Œª‚ÇÅ= -i,

      $$ \begin{aligned} \begin{bmatrix} i & -1 \\ 1 & i \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = 0 \\ \end{aligned} $$

      Let the free variable b=1, then $[^a_b] = [^{-i}_1]$

Repeated eigenvalues

Eigenvalues of a triangular matrix is obvious: the diagonal, as the determinant is directly factorized.

For example, given matrix $ùêÄ = [^{3 \ 1}_{0 \ 3}]$:

  1. Solve its eigenvalues:

    $$ \begin{aligned} | ùêÄ - Œªùêà | = 0 \\ \begin{vmatrix} 3-Œª & 1 \\ 0 & 3-Œª \end{vmatrix} = 0 \\ (3-Œª)(3-Œª) \end{aligned} $$

    Two roots: Œª‚ÇÅ=3, Œª‚ÇÇ= 3

  2. Solve eigenvectors:

    1. For Œª‚ÇÅ=3,

      $$ \begin{aligned} (ùêÄ - Œªùêà) ùêó = 0 \\ \begin{bmatrix} 3-Œª‚ÇÅ & 1 \\ 0 & 3-Œª‚ÇÅ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = 0 \\ \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = 0 \end{aligned} $$

      So $[^a_b]$ can be $[^1_0]$

    2. For Œª‚ÇÇ= 3, same formulas appear. There isn’t the 2nd (set of) independent eigenvectors.

      But there are supposed to be two eigenvalues. It’s incomplete yet.
